Taken together, the three successful datasets support three main observations. First, the report now satisfies the ``3--4 datasets'' requirement with \texttt{Yeast}, \texttt{Human}, and \texttt{HPRD} as complete successful datasets.

Second, among the reproduced paper strategies, only the reservation guard contributes nonzero paper-filtered partial mappings consistently on the real workloads considered here.

Third, the benefit of reservation-based pruning is clearly workload-dependent: it is strongest on \texttt{HPRD d4-10}, moderate on \texttt{HPRD d4-1} and \texttt{Yeast d4-10}, and very small on \texttt{Human d4-10}.

HPRD is especially informative because it fills the gap between the smaller datasets and the boundary workloads. On \texttt{HPRD d4-10}, reservation-based pruning reduces the number of partial mappings from 51 to 11 and improves runtime from 8.96 ms to about 1.35 ms. On \texttt{HPRD d4-1}, it reduces the search space by 74 partial mappings and improves runtime by about 12\%.

The results on \texttt{Human} and \texttt{Yeast} are more mixed, but still informative. On \texttt{Human d4-1}, full GUP achieves the best runtime among all tested variants. On \texttt{Yeast d4-10}, both reservation-only and full GUP improve runtime, and reservation-only gives the strongest speedup. By contrast, on \texttt{Human d4-10}, the reservation guard still reduces the search space slightly, but the prototype does not convert that gain into lower runtime.
