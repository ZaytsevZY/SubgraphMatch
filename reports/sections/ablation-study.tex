The ablation results support three concise conclusions.

First, the \textbf{reservation guard is the dominant effective filtering strategy} in our reproduction (Figure~\ref{fig:ablation}). It is responsible for every nonzero paper-filtered partial-mapping count in the main experiments and for the strongest speedups, especially on \texttt{HPRD d4-10} and \texttt{Yeast d4-10}.

Second, the \textbf{nogood guard remains conservative in pruning power}. In the present implementation it rarely contributes explicit nogood-based pruning on the successful real workloads, which is why its partial-mapping delta is usually zero. Even so, after reducing unnecessary guard checks, the nogood-only variant is not always slower than the baseline; on some lighter workloads it is mildly beneficial in runtime even without visible search-space reduction.

Third, the \textbf{combination of both guards is workload-dependent}. On HPRD and on \texttt{Human d4-1}, full GUP inherits the benefit of reservation-based pruning and outperforms the baseline. On the heavier \texttt{Human d4-10}, however, the prototype still pays more guard overhead than it saves. Overall, the ablation study shows that reservation-based pruning already contributes measurable value, whereas nogood-based pruning still requires a more faithful implementation to demonstrate stable gains.

\subsection{Post-fix Nogood Validation on Broader Queries}

After the original dense-4 ablation, we identified and fixed a cold-start issue in the Nogood implementation: signature lookup was enabled only after a query vertex became active, but the same condition previously prevented the first failed state from being learned. We also removed redundant current-candidate fields from the signature. Under the fixed matching order, the future subproblem is determined by the search depth, used data-vertex set, and frontier mapping. Correctness is checked by the full test suite and by deterministic random differential tests against the baseline.

We then scanned a broader set of real Yeast dense-8 queries and selected two completed queries with nonzero Nogood hits for repeated validation. Table~\ref{tab:nogood-postfix} reports medians after warm-up with rotated execution order. This is a targeted mechanism-validation experiment rather than an unbiased replacement for the six fixed dense-4 workloads; the selection criterion is disclosed because it favors workloads where Nogood is applicable.

The two queries expose complementary behavior. On \texttt{d8-103}, both guards contribute and the full variant achieves the best result. On \texttt{d8-132}, Reservation is almost inactive, while Nogood removes about 56\% of the partial mappings and more than doubles enumeration speed. Together with the fixed dense-4 results, this supports a narrower conclusion than ``one guard always dominates'': Reservation is robust on the original selected workloads, whereas Nogood becomes valuable when many search histories induce the same failing future subproblem.
