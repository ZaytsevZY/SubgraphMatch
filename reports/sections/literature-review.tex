\section{Literature Review and Multi-dimensional Taxonomy}
\label{sec:lit-review}

Exact subgraph matching has been studied from multiple angles, including candidate filtering, matching-order optimization, conflict reuse, and system-level acceleration~\cite{he2008graphql,han2019daf,sun2020study,sun2021rapidmatch,kim2022veq,arai2023gup}. For this course project, the goal of the literature review is not simply to list representative papers, but to construct a \emph{multi-dimensional taxonomy} that can explain where GuP fits and why it is a suitable reproduction target.

\subsection{Taxonomy Dimensions}

We organize the literature along four main dimensions.

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Candidate filtering}: how aggressively a method shrinks the candidate domain of each query vertex before or during the search.
  \item \textbf{Matching order}: whether the method carefully arranges the query-vertex visitation order to expose conflicts earlier.
  \item \textbf{Conflict reuse / failing sets}: whether information from dead ends is remembered and reused to avoid repeating equivalent failures.
  \item \textbf{System-level acceleration}: whether the method relies on heavier indexing, candidate-space engineering, or holistic execution support beyond local pruning logic.
\end{itemize}

These dimensions are complementary rather than mutually exclusive. Many strong systems combine several of them, but different methods emphasize different components.

\subsection{Positioning of GuP}

Under this taxonomy, GuP is best understood as a \emph{search-based exact matcher with dynamic state-aware pruning}. In contrast to purely static filtering methods, GuP attaches pruning logic to the current search state: a reservation guard rejects candidate extensions that violate future injectivity pressure, while a nogood guard attempts to reuse information from discovered dead ends~\cite{arai2023gup}. This places GuP between classical candidate-space methods and failing-set-style conflict reuse methods.

GuP is a particularly suitable course-project target for three reasons. First, its two central ideas---reservation and nogood pruning---are conceptually separable, which makes ablation feasible. Second, compared with highly system-heavy engines, GuP can still be studied meaningfully with a correctness-first prototype. Third, its design allows a direct connection between algorithmic ideas and the four metrics required by the project, especially partial mappings and paper-filtered partial mappings.
