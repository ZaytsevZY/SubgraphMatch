\section{Conclusion and Reflection}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This study examined the core idea of GuP through a correctness-oriented implementation for exact subgraph matching. The paper presents a literature taxonomy, a clear reproduction-scope statement, experiments on three successful datasets, a filtering-strategy ablation study, and a small extension on result reuse for related queries.

From the perspective of the development history of subgraph matching, the field has evolved from earlier candidate-space and access-method ideas~\cite{he2008graphql} toward increasingly integrated systems that combine filtering, order optimization, conflict reuse, and execution engineering~\cite{han2019daf,sun2020study,sun2021rapidmatch}. GuP is an instructive modern point in this development line because it emphasizes dynamic, state-aware pruning while remaining conceptually decomposable into interpretable modules.

Our results lead to four main conclusions. First, the experiments on \texttt{Yeast}, \texttt{Human}, and \texttt{HPRD} cover both lighter and heavier search regimes. Second, reservation-based pruning is the only reproduced GuP mechanism that contributes clear and repeated search-space reductions on the current workloads. Third, the current Python prototype still has a visible scalability boundary, as shown by the boundary runs on \texttt{WordNet} and \texttt{Patents}. Fourth, under a restricted related-query setting, previously materialized result mappings can be reused exactly and can substantially reduce the incremental cost of query refinement.

One clear lesson from the study is that paper reproduction involves more than matching a final table of metrics. It also requires identifying which algorithmic ideas are central, which engineering details control practical performance, and which parts of the original system are simplified in a prototype. The largest gap between GuP and our implementation comes from the guarded candidate-space machinery and the efficiency of the nogood mechanism. This gap helps explain the difference between a sound algorithmic idea and a highly optimized system implementation.

Overall, the study makes the core behavior of GuP visible on real workloads. Guard-based pruning, especially reservation-based pruning, reduces the search space on several queries, while the current results also make clear where a more faithful and more efficient implementation would matter most. The result-reuse extension further suggests that workload-level reuse is a promising complementary direction: later queries in a refinement sequence can benefit from results that have already been computed.
